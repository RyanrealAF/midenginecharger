Aerodynamics & Downforce Strategy

The primary goal is to achieve an optimal L/D (Lift-to-Drag) ratio, prioritizing high-speed stability and grip without incurring excessive parasitic drag. This section details the computational fluid dynamics (CFD) findings and component specifications.

Computational Modeling & Targets

Initial CFD simulations target a maximum lift coefficient ($C_L$) of -1.2 at 150 mph, balanced with a minimal increase in the drag coefficient ($C_D$).

  • Target Downforce: 450 lbs @ 150 mph.
  • Target $C_D$ Increase: < 8% over the baseline chassis.

The flow must remain attached across the rear wing element across a 15-degree angle of attack range to ensure stability under braking and corner entry.

Front Element: Splitter & Canards

The front splitter is constructed from pre-preg carbon fiber (see: Materials for layup details) with a 6-inch projection. This acts as a kinetic boundary layer control device.

Canard Configuration

Dual-element canards are employed to generate strong vortices that manage wheel-well turbulence and feed clean air to the side-skirts. The angle of attack for the primary canard is fixed at 12 degrees.

The formula for vortex generation potential $VGP$ is given by:

$$ VGP = \frac{\rho V^2 A C_L \sin(\alpha)}{2} $$

where $\rho$ is air density, $V$ is velocity, $A$ is the canard area, $C_L$ is the lift coefficient, and $\alpha$ is the angle of attack.

Rear Diffuser Geometry

A low-pressure zone is generated beneath the vehicle using a dual-tunnel diffuser. The critical element is the expansion ratio (ER).

  • Throat Height: 80mm
  • Exit Height: 250mm
  • Expansion Ratio (ER): 3.125

Proper airflow separation must be maintained at the diffuser exit, preventing flow detachment. We utilize small boundary layer energizers (vanelets) within the tunnels to stabilize flow at high yaw angles.
